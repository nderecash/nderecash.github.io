I arrived in Tampere in September 2024 with two suitcases, a residence permit, and a very incomplete picture of what I was getting into.

I'd done research. I'd read the guides. I knew about the dark winters, the quiet public culture, the sauna etiquette. What I hadn't understood was how much of settling into a new country is not the things you prepare for, but the things that catch you sideways.

This is not a complaints post. Moving to Finland is one of the better decisions I've made. But I think the guides written for international students tend toward reassurance in a way that isn't always useful. So here's an honest version.

---

## The cold is not what you think

The cold isn't the problem. I prepared for the cold. I bought the right layers, I learned the rules about dressing in threes, I understood intellectually that there would be days when it didn't get light.

The problem is the short days. In December, in Tampere, sunrise is around 9am and sunset is around 3pm. That's six hours of daylight on a clear day. In practice, between cloud cover and the time you spend indoors, you can go several days without seeing the sun.

This is manageable. It's also genuinely strange, and the strangeness doesn't fully go away by January. You adapt to it, but you don't stop noticing it.

I bought a daylight lamp in November. It helped more than I expected.

---

## Finnish social dynamics take time

Finnish culture has a reputation for reserve, and it's not inaccurate. This is not coldness — it is a different calibration of what social interaction is for, and at what pace it develops.

What I found was that people were, almost without exception, kind when asked for help. The difficulty was that the help-offering that feels natural to me — the preemptive check-in, the small talk — isn't how things work here. You're not going to make friends at the bus stop.

The friendships I've made in Tampere have been slower to start and more solid once they exist than in most other contexts I've been in. I think that's the trade-off. It takes longer to get there, and when you do, there's more there.

International student networks help enormously — ESN in particular. But if you come expecting the social density of, say, a West African city, you will need to recalibrate your expectations and your patience.

---

## The bureaucracy is complicated but not hostile

Finnish bureaucracy for immigrants involves a number of moving parts: the residence permit, the personal identity code (henkilötunnus), bank account access, Kela (the social insurance system), and the tax card. Each one has dependencies on the others, and the order matters.

The good news is that the systems are clear and the people working in them are, in my experience, patient and helpful. The bad news is that you can easily lose six to eight weeks to the sequence — particularly the bank account, which some banks won't open until you have a henkilötunnus, which takes time to process.

If you're coming to Finland for a programme, sort your banking before you arrive if you can. N26 or Wise can bridge the gap while you wait for a Finnish account.

---

## The university is genuinely excellent

Tampere University is good. The resources, the research environment, the supervisors I've worked with — all good. PIRG has been a place where I've actually been able to do the work I came here to do, which is not always guaranteed in academic environments.

The student culture is quieter than I expected — less of the social intensity I associated with university in Malawi — but the academic engagement is serious and the expectations are clear.

One thing I wasn't prepared for: the independence. Finnish academic culture assumes you can manage your own time, set your own pace, identify your own problems, and ask for help when you need it rather than waiting to be guided. If you're used to a more structured environment, this is an adjustment.

I found it suits me, once I stopped waiting for someone to tell me what to do next.

---

## The part that doesn't go away

The part that nobody fully prepares you for is being far from home in a way that is genuinely irreducible.

I left Malawi at 28. I have a family there. I have friendships that are fifteen years old. None of them are here. Zoom calls work, but they don't fully work, and the time difference between Tampere and Lilongwe is two hours — close enough that you should be able to overlap, far enough that you often don't.

This isn't a reason not to go. It's a real cost. Naming it honestly isn't pessimism; it's just accurate accounting.

The work is good. The environment is good. I don't regret it. I also don't pretend it's cost-free.

If you're thinking about it — come. But pack the good headphones, buy the lamp, and make peace with the fact that some things take longer here than you expect.
